The Story of Michael van Gerwen

For the better part of a decade, if you wanted to win a darts tournament, you had to get past Michael van Gerwen first. And good luck with that. The bald-headed, green-shirted Dutchman didn't just beat people — he steamrollered them, often before they'd had time to find their rhythm.

Born on 25 April 1989 in Boxtel and raised in nearby Vlijmen, van Gerwen actually started out as a footballer, playing as a defender until he was 12. Then he picked up a set of darts at 13 and the football boots got binned soon after. The talent was instant and frightening. At 14 he reached the final of a youth Masters event, and by 17 he'd done something nobody had ever managed — he won the 2006 Winmau World Masters, snatching Eric Bristow's record as the youngest ever champion at 17 years and 174 days.

The famous fist-pump — van Gerwen's intensity and celebrations are all part of the Mighty Mike package.

The hype was off the charts. But darts is a cruel game, and the early breakthrough didn't translate straight away. Van Gerwen switched to the PDC in 2007 and then spent a few wilderness years struggling for consistency, even losing the PDC World Youth final in both 2011 and 2012. Ranked world number 38 at the start of 2012, plenty had written him off as a kid who peaked too soon.

Then the floodgates opened. In 2013 he won his first PDC major at the World Grand Prix and reached the World Championship final. In 2014, aged 24, he became the then-youngest PDC World Champion — and rocketed to world number one, a spot he'd own from 2014 right through to 2021. Three world titles followed (2014, 2017, 2019), a record seven Premier League crowns, six World Grand Prix titles, and a haul of 48 PDC major singles titles that sits second on the all-time list behind only Phil Taylor.

He owns the most absurd stat in the sport: a 123.40 televised average against Michael Smith in the 2016 Premier League — the highest ever recorded, and a game he very nearly turned into a whitewash, missing double 18 three times for what would have been an average north of 130.

Even now, ranked world number four and with the next generation snapping at his heels, MVG remains box office. When that "Seven Nation Army" riff kicks in and the crowd starts chanting his name, you know you're watching one of the all-time greats. The Michael van Gerwen darts story is far from over.

What Darts Does Michael van Gerwen Use?

This is the question every MVG fan wants answered. Michael van Gerwen throws 23g Winmau MvG signature darts — the EvoX Professional range, machined from 90% tungsten. After years of playing Winmau, the barrel has been fine-tuned over multiple generations to suit exactly how he plays: fast, front-loaded, and relentlessly heavy on the scoring.

The barrel carries a series of precision grip cuts that give van Gerwen instant, repeatable purchase without slowing his lightning release. He's one of the quickest throwers in the game, so the grip has to grab cleanly and let go cleanly — anything that snags would wreck his tempo. The 90% tungsten construction keeps the barrel slim for its weight, which means tighter grouping in a packed treble bed. That's how MVG darts have helped him pile in those famous purple patches.

Playing Style & Technique

Van Gerwen leans well forward over the oche and throws at a ferocious pace, scoring so heavily that he can build a winning lead in a matter of seconds. That's the essence of the Green Machine — momentum. He gets on top of opponents fast and rarely lets them breathe.

Michael van Gerwen in full flow — the unmistakable green shirt that earned him the nickname 'The Green Machine'.

His style is instinctive and natural rather than mechanical. When he does miss, he's brilliant at parking it and refocusing on the next leg — no sulking, no spiral. And then there are the "purple patches": those frightening spells where he instantly elevates his game to a level almost nobody can live with. Five-time World Champion Eric Bristow described him as fearless, and van Gerwen himself has said he's not scared of any player on the planet.

The celebrations are part of the package too — short, sharp fist-pumps and the odd headbutt to the air when an important visit lands. It's pure intensity, and it's exactly why crowds chant "Oh, Michael van Gerwen" to that Seven Nation Army riff.
