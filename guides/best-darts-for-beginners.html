🎯 Buying Guide — How to Choose the Right Darts as a Beginner

Choosing your first proper set of darts can feel overwhelming — there are hundreds of options at different prices, weights and materials. Here's everything you need to know to make the right choice.

Tungsten vs Brass — Why It Matters

⚡
The #1 Rule
Always buy tungsten. Never buy brass.
This is the single most important decision when choosing darts.

Tungsten is a much denser metal than brass. This means a tungsten barrel can be significantly slimmer than a brass barrel at the same weight. Slimmer barrels take up less space on the board, which means your darts can group tighter together — especially important when you're aiming at the triple 20.

Brass darts are cheap for a reason. They're fat, they wear out quickly, the grip pattern fades after a few weeks, and they physically limit your scoring potential because the barrels are too wide to fit three darts in a tight cluster.

Feature Brass Tungsten
Barrel width Fat — wide barrel at any weight Slim — narrow barrel at same weight
Grouping potential Limited — barrels block each other Excellent — slim barrels fit close together
Durability Grip wears off in weeks Grip lasts months or years
Price $5 – $15 $30 – $80+
Our verdict ❌ Avoid ✅ Always buy

What Tungsten Percentage Should You Get?

Tungsten darts come in different percentages — 80%, 85%, 90%, 95% and above. The percentage refers to how much of the barrel is pure tungsten, with the rest being nickel or other metals. Higher percentage = denser = slimmer barrel.

Percentage Barrel Width Price Range Best For
80% Standard $30 – $55 Beginners on a budget
85% Slightly slimmer $35 – $55 Best balance of price and performance
90% Noticeably slim $45 – $80 Serious beginners and improvers
95% Very slim $60 – $100+ Competitive players wanting the slimmest profile

Our recommendation for beginners: 80% or 90% tungsten. 80% is perfectly fine for learning and won't hold you back. 90% is the sweet spot if you want to invest a bit more — you'll feel the difference in how slim the barrel is and how tight your grouping gets as you improve.

What Weight Should You Choose?

⚖️
Best Weight for Beginners
22g – 24g
This is the most popular weight range for beginners and professionals alike. Start here and adjust later.

Dart weight is personal. There's no "correct" weight — it depends on your throwing style, grip and how the dart feels in your hand. However, there are some general guidelines:

  • Under 20g (light) — requires more force and a faster throw to reach the board with a flat trajectory. Tends to suit players with a quick, snappy release
  • 20g – 22g (light-medium) — a good starting point for players who prefer a lighter feel. Requires moderate force and offers good control
  • 22g – 24g (medium — recommended) — the sweet spot for most players. Heavy enough to carry momentum to the board without excessive force, light enough to maintain control and accuracy. Most professionals throw in this range
  • 24g – 26g (heavy) — suits players who prefer a more substantial feel and a slower, more deliberate throw. The extra weight helps the dart maintain a straight flight path
  • Over 26g (very heavy) — less common but preferred by some players. Can be tiring over long sessions but offers excellent stability. The Viper Blitz on this list goes up to 28g for heavy throwers

The best advice: Start with 22g or 23g. Play for a few weeks. If the darts feel too light and float too much, go heavier. If they feel too heavy and drop before reaching the board, go lighter. Most darts on this list offer multiple weight options so you can choose based on your preference.

Understanding Grip Patterns

The grip pattern on a dart barrel is what gives your fingers traction during the throw. Different patterns suit different grips and throwing styles:

  • Ring grip — a series of horizontal rings cut into the barrel. Provides consistent, even grip across the entire holding area. The most common and beginner-friendly pattern. Used by the Winmau Joe Cullen and MvG Aspire on this list
  • Radial groove grip — precision-milled cuts that run around the barrel at specific angles. Provides a slightly more aggressive grip than ring patterns. Used by the Target Hydro and Nathan Aspinall on this list
  • Scalloped grip — curved indentations that naturally guide your fingers into position. Excellent for beginners because it encourages the same grip every throw. Used by the Viper Blitz on this list
  • Knurled grip — a cross-hatched diamond pattern that provides aggressive grip. Can be too sticky for some players and may cause the dart to hang on your fingers during release. Not featured on this list as it's less suitable for beginners

Our recommendation: For beginners, a medium ring grip or radial groove grip is ideal. It provides enough grip to hold the dart confidently without being so aggressive that the dart sticks to your fingers on release.

What Comes in the Box?

Every dart on this list comes with everything you need to start throwing immediately — barrels, shafts and flights. Here's what each component does:

  • Barrels — the main tungsten body of the dart that you hold. This is the most important (and expensive) component. Always comes in a set of three
  • Shafts (stems) — the piece that connects the barrel to the flight. Available in different lengths — short shafts give a faster, more direct flight; long shafts give more stability. Most beginners should start with short or medium shafts
  • Flights — the "wings" at the back of the dart that stabilise it in the air. Standard shape flights are the most popular and the most beginner-friendly. Flights are consumable — they'll get damaged over time and need replacing
  • Points (tips) — the metal point at the front. Fixed on most darts, interchangeable on the Target Hydro with Swiss Point technology

Essential Accessories

These aren't included with most darts but are well worth buying alongside your first set:

  • Dart case or wallet — protects your darts when not in use and during transport. The Target Aspinall and Viper Blitz include one; for the others you'll need to buy separately (~$10-15)
  • Spare flights — flights take the most punishment and will need replacing regularly. Buy a multipack — a set of 3 flights costs around $3-5
  • Dart sharpener — keeps your points sharp for clean entry into the board. A blunt point causes more bounce-outs and damages the sisal. A basic sharpener costs around $5
  • Flight protectors — small metal or plastic caps that sit on top of the flight to protect it from incoming darts. Extends flight life significantly (~$3)

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

What weight darts should a beginner use?

Most beginners should start with 22g – 24g darts. This is the most popular weight range among all players — from beginners to professionals. 22g-23g offers a good balance of control and forgiveness, while 24g gives a bit more momentum for players who prefer a heavier throw. If you're unsure, go with 23g — it's the middle ground that works for almost everyone.

What percentage tungsten should beginners get?

80% tungsten is the sweet spot for beginners on a budget. It gives you a slimmer barrel than brass at an affordable price. 90% tungsten is even slimmer and more durable but costs more. Both are excellent choices. Avoid brass darts — they're fat, wear out fast and hold you back from day one.

Should beginners buy player edition darts?

Yes — player edition darts from major brands like Winmau, Target and Red Dragon are excellent quality and designed by professional players. They're not gimmicks. Players like Peter Wright, Michael van Gerwen and Nathan Aspinall have genuine input into the barrel shape, grip pattern and weight distribution. Plus, there's something motivating about throwing the same dart as a world champion.

Are expensive darts worth it for beginners?

You don't need to spend over $80 as a beginner, but spending $40-70 on a quality tungsten set is absolutely worth it. The difference between a $15 brass set and a $50 tungsten set is night and day — better grip, slimmer barrels, tighter grouping and they last years instead of months. Think of it this way: a $60 dart set that lasts 2 years costs less than $3 per month.

What's the difference between tungsten and brass darts?

Tungsten is denser than brass, which means tungsten barrels can be much slimmer at the same weight. Slimmer barrels allow tighter grouping on the board. Tungsten is also more durable and maintains its grip pattern much longer. Brass darts are cheaper but fatter, wear out faster and limit your scoring potential.

Do I need to buy flights and shafts separately?

No — all seven darts on this list come with flights and shafts included. You can throw them straight out of the box. However, flights and shafts are consumable parts that wear out over time, so you'll eventually want to buy replacements. They're cheap — a set of flights costs around $3-5.

What dartboard should I use with these darts?

All seven darts on this list are steel tip darts designed for sisal bristle dartboards. We recommend the Winmau Blade 6 — the official board of the PDC used in every major tournament. It's around $60-70 and will last years. Read our full guide: Best Dart Boards 2026.

How do I set up my dartboard properly?

The bullseye must be 5ft 8in (1.73m) from the floor and the throwing line should be 7ft 9¼in (2.37m) from the face of the board. Getting these measurements right from day one is crucial for building correct muscle memory. Read our full setup guide: Dart Board Distance & Height Guide.

How do I keep score?

Most beginners start with a game of 501 — each player starts at 501 points and works down to zero by subtracting the score of each throw. The twist is you must finish on a double. It sounds complicated but it's simple once you understand the board layout. Read our full guide: How to Score Darts.
