Buying Guide — How to Choose the Right Darts as a Beginner

There are hundreds of darts out there and everyone's got an opinion. Here's what actually matters when you're choosing your first proper set — no fluff, no jargon.

Tungsten vs Brass — The Only Rule That Matters

⚡
The #1 Rule
Always buy tungsten. Never buy brass.
Seriously. This is the single most important decision.

Here's the simple version. Tungsten is denser than brass. Denser means the barrel can be slimmer at the same weight. Slimmer barrels take up less space on the board, which means your darts can group tighter together — especially around treble 20 where it matters most.

Brass darts are cheap for a reason. They're fat, the grip wears off in weeks, and they physically stop you from fitting three darts close together. You're handicapping yourself from throw one. Don't do it.

Feature Brass Tungsten
Barrel width Fat — wide barrel at any weight Slim — narrow barrel at same weight
Grouping potential Limited — barrels block each other Excellent — slim barrels fit close together
Durability Grip wears off in weeks Grip lasts months or years
Price $5 – $15 $30 – $80+
Our verdict ❌ Avoid ✅ Always buy

What Tungsten Percentage Should You Get?

Tungsten darts come in different percentages — 80%, 85%, 90%, 95% and above. Higher percentage means more tungsten, denser barrel, slimmer profile. But the price goes up too. Here's the honest breakdown:

Percentage Barrel Width Price Range Best For
80% Standard $30 – $55 Beginners on a budget
85% Slightly slimmer $35 – $55 Best balance of price and performance
90% Noticeably slim $45 – $80 Serious beginners who want to invest
95% Very slim $60 – $100+ Players wanting the slimmest possible profile
💡 Our Take

80% is perfectly fine for learning and won't hold you back. 90% is the sweet spot if you're happy spending a bit more — you'll notice the difference in how slim the barrel feels and how tight your grouping gets as you improve. Don't stress about this too much though. Any tungsten is better than brass.

What Weight Should You Choose?

⚖️
Best Weight for Beginners
22g – 24g
Most popular range for beginners and professionals. Start here and adjust if needed.

Weight is personal. There's no "right" answer — it depends on how you throw and what feels natural. But here are some rough guidelines:

  • Under 20g (light) — needs more force and a quicker throw. Suits people with a fast, snappy release
  • 20g – 22g (light-medium) — good if you prefer a lighter feel. Moderate force, good control
  • 22g – 24g (medium — start here) — the sweet spot for most people. Heavy enough to carry to the board, light enough to control. Most pros throw in this range
  • 24g – 26g (heavy) — suits a slower, more deliberate throw. The weight helps the dart fly straight with less effort
  • Over 26g (very heavy) — less common but some people love it. Can tire your arm over long sessions. The Viper Blitz goes up to 28g if this is you
💡 Honest Advice

Start with 22g or 23g. Throw for a few weeks. If the darts feel floaty and don't do what you want, go heavier. If your arm gets tired and they feel like rocks, go lighter. Don't overthink it — you'll know when it feels right.

Understanding Grip Patterns

The grip pattern is what stops the dart slipping out of your fingers. Different patterns suit different people — there's no best one, just what feels right for you:

  • Ring grip — horizontal rings cut into the barrel. Consistent, even grip that works for most people. The most common and beginner-friendly pattern. Used by the Joe Cullen and MvG Aspire on this list
  • Radial groove — precision-milled angular cuts. Slightly more aggressive than ring grip but very consistent. Used by the Target Hydro and Nathan Aspinall
  • Scalloped grip — curved indentations that guide your fingers into position. Brilliant for consistency because your fingers naturally sit in the same place every time. Used by the Viper Blitz
  • Knurled grip — cross-hatched diamond pattern. Very aggressive grip. Can feel too sticky and cause the dart to hang on your fingers during release. Not on this list — it's not ideal for beginners

What Comes in the Box?

Every dart on this list is ready to throw straight away. Here's what each piece does:

  • Barrels — the main tungsten body. This is what you hold and what costs the money. Always comes in a set of three
  • Shafts (stems) — connects the barrel to the flight. Short shafts give a faster, flatter flight. Long shafts give more stability. Start with short or medium
  • Flights — the "wings" at the back that keep the dart stable in the air. Standard shape is the most popular and best for beginners. These get damaged over time and need replacing — they're cheap though
  • Points (tips) — the metal point at the front. Fixed on most darts, swappable on the Target Hydro with Swiss Point

Stuff Worth Buying Alongside Your Darts

Not essential on day one, but you'll want these eventually:

🧳

Dart Case or Wallet

Keeps your darts protected and together. The Aspinall and Viper Blitz include one. For the rest, you'll need to buy your own. Worth it to stop your points getting blunted in a drawer.

🪶

Spare Flights

Flights take the most abuse. They'll get ripped, bent and battered by incoming darts. Buy a multipack — about $3-5 for a set of 3. You'll go through them.

🔧

Dart Sharpener

Blunt points bounce off the board and chew up the sisal. A quick touch-up on a sharpener keeps them entering cleanly. About $5 and lasts ages.

🛡️

Flight Protectors

Little caps that sit on top of your flights to stop incoming darts splitting them. About $3 and they genuinely extend flight life. Worth it.

Frequently Asked Questions

What weight darts should a beginner use?

Start with 22g – 24g. That's the range most people settle on, beginners and pros alike. If you've got no idea, go 23g — it's the safest middle ground. If they feel too floaty after a few sessions, go heavier next time. If they feel like rocks, go lighter. You'll figure it out quickly.

What percentage tungsten should beginners get?

80% tungsten is the sweet spot if you're watching the budget. Slimmer than brass, affordable, does the job well. 90% tungsten is noticeably slimmer and lasts longer but costs more. Both are solid choices. Just avoid brass — it's fat, wears out fast and will hold you back from day one.

Should beginners buy player edition darts?

Go for it. Player edition darts from Winmau, Target and Red Dragon are genuinely well-made. Players like Peter Wright, Michael van Gerwen and Nathan Aspinall have real input into the design — barrel shape, grip, weight distribution. These aren't just generic darts with a name slapped on. Plus there's something motivating about throwing the same darts as a world champion.

Are expensive darts worth it for beginners?

You don't need to spend over $80. But spending $40-70 on a proper tungsten set is absolutely worth it. The difference between a $15 brass set and a $50 tungsten set is night and day — better grip, slimmer barrels, tighter grouping, and they'll last years instead of weeks. A $60 set that lasts 2 years costs less than $3 a month. Bargain.

What's the difference between tungsten and brass darts?

Tungsten is denser, so the barrels can be much slimmer at the same weight. Slimmer barrels mean your darts group tighter on the board. Tungsten also lasts way longer — the grip stays sharp for months instead of wearing smooth in weeks. Brass is cheaper but the barrels are fat, the grip disappears fast, and they physically limit how well you can score.

Do I need to buy flights and shafts separately?

Nope. All seven darts on this list come with flights and shafts in the box — ready to throw straight away. But flights and shafts do wear out over time, so you'll want spares eventually. They're cheap though — a set of flights is about $3-5.

What dartboard should I use with these darts?

All seven darts on this list are steel tip darts, so you need a sisal bristle dartboard. We recommend the Winmau Blade 6 — it's the official board of the PDC, used in every major tournament, and costs around $60-70. It'll last you years. Full review here: Best Dart Boards 2026.

How do I set up my dartboard properly?

Bullseye goes at 5ft 8in (1.73m) from the floor. Throwing line at 7ft 9¼in (2.37m) from the board face. Get these right from the start — practising at the wrong distance builds bad muscle memory. Full setup guide here: Dart Board Distance & Height.

How do I keep score?

Most beginners start with 501 — you start at 501 points and subtract each throw until you reach exactly zero. The catch is you have to finish on a double. It sounds tricky but it clicks fast once you understand the board layout. Full guide here: How to Score Darts.
